Pongclock

Pongclock was borne out of my frustrations with, and the death of Chumby. It's an Arduino-compatible clock with a 1.8" 160x128 tft display and a Maxim DS1307 to keep time.

Schematic, PCB, Code, Faceplate STL

Programming the ATmega328

I made a little breadboard-compatible programming jig with pogo pins and some little 5x5 protoboards. This allowed me to use a spare arduino nano and ArduinoISP to get the arduino bootloader onto pongclock without soldering on a header.

Faceplate

I had planned on designing a full case, but ended up printing only the faceplate as a test. The fit was perfect, and I liked the look of the exposed PCB, so I slapped some 8mm M3 bolts on it and called it done.

The faceplate was printed by a Gigabot via the "airbnb of 3d printing," makexyz. The service allows you to find idle 3d printers in your area. I've had some mixed results from different vendors, but the folks at Gigabot delivered a beautiful print very rapidly.

Rubber feet and/or a deeper base/stand might be good additions, as it really likes to slide and fall backwards on my desk.

PCB Fabrication

The PCB design files were sent off to OSH Park where they were batched up with other customers, panelized, and sent off to a top secret fabrication facility. For small quantities of small boards, their prices can't be beat. The resulting boards in OSH Park's signature purple solder mask are gorgeous. If you look closely, you can see that my third order (bottom right) must have made at a different fab. There's a noticeable difference in the solder mask color, but the silk screen quality is vastly improved.

Previous Iterations

I knew almost nothing about electronics and PCB design going into this project. I learned a great deal along the way with the help of some very kind members of the electronics community at /r/electronics and /r/ece.

The most important things I learned were the value of actually reading datasheets, and triple-checking my work.

Pongclock 1.0

It took about 8 iterations to get to this point. I was still learning how to use Diptrace, figuring out the final form factor, how I was going to power the thing, and finally flirting with a few surface mount components.


In this version, I simply attached a breakout board from sainsmart for the LCD with some wire.

Can you spot the big mistake? Look a little closer at the battery. You can see that I had to wrap the vertical sections of the battery holder with heat-shrink and flip the battery upside down. Oops! What you probably can't see is that in additional to the battery polarity, I've also accidentally reversed the rx/tx lines on the FTDI header. Also visible is a pretty horrendous soldering job.

Pongclock 2.0

Things are getting a little more exciting here. The electronics from the original breakout board (minus the sd card slot) have joined the party. The clock crystal now has a nice little ground moat, and a solder pad for the casing. A third switch, along with a transistor and a couple resistors allow you to dim the backlight. And we're now almost completely surface-mount. I credit Dave Jones' videos with giving the the confidence I needed to solder a TQFP.

The screenshot above was taken taken after a round of changes suggested by a reddit user. You can see that the routing is still very inefficient. It's easy to see in retrospect where certain components could be physically moved, or pins swapped to clean this up. The ground plane on the reverse side is all broken up. Yuck.

There's also a few major problems with this version that I wouldn't discover until after I had completely assembled it. How exactly do I get the arduino bootloader onto the thing? I soldered some small wires onto the leads of the ICs. How do I mount the LCD with the leads of the 16mhz crystal poking through to that side of the board? I got out the dremel tool. These oversights made for a time consuming assembly process, so another iteration was in order.

Pongclock 2.1

I've fixed the glaring issues with the previous board, swapped in a smaller battery holder, and added a fourth (unused for now) button. With two buttons on each side of the board, an actual game of pong between humans could be played. I've even moved the silkscreen labels for the FTDI header so that you can read them even after the header is installed.
